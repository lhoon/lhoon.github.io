Dynamic Pricing

Cover Genius

Involvement: Data Scientist, Software Engineer


At Cover Genius, we designed and launched the world's first dynamic pricing engine for rental car insurance. Running off our bespoke A/B and Multivariate testing framework, this system helped us detect pricing sensitivity across individual variables. In effect, we raised B2C and B2B conversion, increasing gross revenue by 15%.



The Dementia Friendly Home

Deakin Software and Technology Innovation Laboratory (DSTIL)

Involvement: Usability Analyst (version 1)


Alzheimer’s Australia engaged us to develop an app to educate carers about Dementia Enabling Environment Principles through manipulation of a 3D modelled home. These changes can be effected with annotated rationale and consequences to allow people living with dementia to maintain their independence at home.



I performed heuristic evaluations and ran usability tests for this app. Our primary users were identified to be family members and carers of persons living with dementia. The primary goal of this app enabled users to understand the effects of positive furnishing changes that could be made to their households. We invested significant time in refining the user experience as our primary users were typically only moderately proficient with tablet usage, and typically not proficient with 3D exploration that is more commonly used in games.

Trauma Reception & Resuscitation

Swinburne NICTA Software Innovation Lab (SSIL)

Involvement: Researcher


The Trauma Reception & Resuscitation project is a Decision Support System built at the SSIL for The Alfred’s Trauma Centre. This system is currently deployed in the Trauma Bays of The Alfred and also enables the trauma team the capability to enrich the algorithmic knowledge base of the system with minor training.



Users have noted the affordance for greater accountability during surgery. Additionally, the display of timeline since arrival and procedures performed provided the team members critical contextual information that augments the patient's current vital signs, especially when arriving in the bay separately.



Since deployment, an 80% reduction in blood administered to patients, 21% reduction in errors of omission and 3 hours less time spent in trauma have been observed. I produced one conference and one journal publication about affordances enabled by this system.

Extracting Signal from the Noise of App Reviews


From my thesis: Extracting Signal from the Noise of App Reviews


Brief Abstract

Apps stores offer a transparent feedback loop between users and developers via public app reviews and direct distribution of updates to users. Significant effort is expended to glean useful information from reviews and to examine this feedback loop. This thesis uses statistical and text analysis on 8.7 million reviews to demonstrate that the majority of reviews, when read in isolation, do not yield developers much actionable feedback. Reviews are analysed to offer app benchmarking, search space reduction techniques and typical review properties to detect and sort useful reviews, toward informing project expectations, priorities and scoping.



Full Abstract

The App Store and Google Play are mobile app store fronts furnished with a public review system. The competition on these store fronts is intense– developers can push new releases to users directly; users can publicly express opinions on the apps they have used. The resulting transparent feedback loop, while evident to the app community, is visible to competitors as well. Studying these app reviews within a domain can inform the design of their own competing app, exploiting any prior user dissatisfaction. Thus, it behoves developers to maintain quality, in either attempts to enter this market with a new app or efforts in continuous refinement of an existing app portfolio.



Opinion mining research has yielded observations of interest to businesses by analysing user reviews of their products. These research efforts leverage static analysis techniques and natural language processing approaches to discern generalisable patterns and analyse content. However, a good framework to mine mobile app reviews for content useful to app developers is lacking. Our work addresses this gap.



In this thesis we demonstrate that the majority of reviews when read in isolation, do not yield developers much actionable feedback. However, using a series of analytics, we can construct a frame of context to detect developer-useful information. More specifically, our work analyses 8.7 million reviews from 17,330 apps at a store–, category– and app-level perspective. We present a statistically constructed app review model that profiles review properties, growth and evolution. A text stabilisation treatment is also offered to prepare reviews for content analysis. Through use of the model to reduce the content search space, we offer a technique to detect and sort reviews by content usefulness. Holistic application of these analytics forms an app development benchmarking framework to inform project expectations, priorities and scoping.
